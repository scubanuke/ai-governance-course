# The Four-Filter Applicability Gate

> **Part of:** Bridge / Module 5

Carving a facility class tells you the threat profile for a *type* of facility. It does not yet tell you whether a *particular* facility is actually in scope. Those are different questions, and confusing them is how governance frameworks end up either drowning every site in requirements it doesn't need or quietly excusing the one site that mattered. This unit is the gate that separates the two questions: the **four-filter applicability gate.**

## Class membership is not facility exposure

Start with the premise the gate rests on. **Class membership** establishes what a military-capable adversary *could* do to a facility of a given kind — the threat profile of the type. **Facility-specific exposure** asks whether that threat profile is actually credible for the specific facility in front of you. A generation plant deep in the continental interior with no strategic significance carries a very different real exposure than a substation anchoring a defense-critical corridor near a military installation or coastline — even when the two share a nominally severe class profile.

The gate exists to answer the second question. It is applied to the specific facility, not to its class, and it produces a recorded determination of whether the (military-action) design basis applies here.

## The four filters

Each filter is applied to the facility itself. Any one of them can bring the facility into scope.

**Filter 1 — Geolocation relative to defense-critical loads and supply.** Does the facility serve, directly or through the network it participates in, a demand or supply cluster that includes military installations, national-security infrastructure, or continuity-of-government functions? In electricity this is a defense-critical load cluster; in oil and gas it is a defense-fuel supply or strategic-logistics commitment. Proximity or contribution to defense-critical loads elevates applicability regardless of the facility's own strategic profile.

**Filter 2 — Position in strategic transport corridors.** Does the facility sit on or anchor a strategic corridor, chokepoint, or coastline — a position that gives it significance beyond its individual output?

**Filter 3 — Substitutability.** If this facility were lost, how quickly and by what means could its function be replaced — redispatch, rerouting, mutual aid, alternate supply, drawdown? A facility whose loss cannot be compensated within the restoration timeline carries higher design-basis relevance regardless of geolocation. Low substitutability raises applicability on its own.

**Filter 4 — Adversary targeting logic.** Would a state-level adversary, applying the targeting criteria in the threat characterization, identify this facility as a node worth including in a *pattern* attack? This filter forces the operator to reason from the adversary's vantage, not their own. A facility that looks modest in isolation may be a high-value node because of its position in a pattern rather than its individual consequence.

## The combination rule — and why it is asymmetric

The four filters are **not** a checklist to be scored and summed, and this is the most important thing to understand about the gate.

For a **positive** determination, the gate is **disjunctive**: any single filter satisfied is sufficient to bring the facility into scope. Serve a defense-critical load, *or* sit on a strategic corridor, *or* be non-substitutable within the restoration timeline, *or* be a pattern-attack node — any one puts the facility in. The filters do not offset one another; a negative reading on three does not cancel a positive on the fourth.

For a **negative** determination — concluding the design basis does *not* apply — the reasoning is cumulative: the facility must clear *all four* filters, with none of them individually bringing it into scope. Only the negative conclusion is weight-of-evidence, and only the negative conclusion is subject to a documented-and-reviewable test.

That asymmetry is deliberate, and it encodes a judgment worth absorbing: **the gate is built to fail toward inclusion.** The cost of wrongly *including* a facility is some effort spent on a design basis it may not have strictly needed. The cost of wrongly *excluding* one is borne under military-action conditions, when the mistake can no longer be corrected. Faced with that trade, the gate leans, on purpose, toward scoping a facility *in.*

## The determination is documented, not unilateral

An operator who concludes the design basis does not apply must document that conclusion, address each of the four filters explicitly in the reasoning, and retain it for review. This is not a self-issued exemption. It is a reviewable professional judgment, assessable on its merits against each filter. Bringing a facility *in* needs one filter; keeping it *out* needs a defended, on-the-record clearance of all four.

## A reusable gate, not an energy artifact

Although this gate originated in the electric instance and is instantiated by the energy sector frameworks, it is written as shared, sector-neutral machinery: a canonical module that any sector's instrument can cite rather than restate. Read it that way. The four filters — defense-critical nexus, strategic corridor position, substitutability, adversary targeting logic — and the disjunctive-in / cumulative-out combination rule are portable to any critical-infrastructure sector deciding whether a facility falls within a design basis. That portability is exactly what lets you apply it to your own sector next.

## In your sector

Take the candidate facility you have been developing. Run the four filters against it, one at a time, and notice how few you need: does it touch a defense-critical load, sit on a strategic corridor, resist substitution, or read as a pattern-attack node? If any one holds, it is in scope — and you have just performed the applicability determination the method requires. Then try the harder direction: could you write a defensible, reviewable statement that it clears *all four* and is genuinely out? Feel which determination the evidence makes easy.
