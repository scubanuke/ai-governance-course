# What a Facility Class Is

> **Part of:** Bridge / Module 5

You have the method. The core taught you to derive a design basis, recognize that an AI operating in a facility needs its own, and build the machinery that holds the seam between them. This module is the bridge, and its job is to answer a question the core deliberately postponed: *to what do you apply the method?* The answer is a **facility class**, and this unit defines it. This is where the sector you chose back in Module 0 finally stops being cosmetic.

## The concept

A **facility class** is a category of facilities alike enough — in the consequences they can produce and the adversary envelope they face — that a single design basis can serve the whole category rather than being rebuilt one plant at a time. Instead of deriving a fresh design basis for every LNG terminal on the coast, you carve the *class* "LNG import/export terminal," derive the design basis once at the level of the class, and then apply and tailor it to each member facility. The class is the reusable unit of the method.

This matters because it is what makes the discipline scalable. Deriving a design basis is demanding work. If it had to be done wholly from scratch for every facility, it would never keep pace with the infrastructure it is meant to protect — the founding gap from Module 0 would simply reappear as a throughput problem. The facility class solves that: do the hard consequence-and-adversary reasoning once for the category, and every facility in the category inherits a design basis it only has to adapt, not invent.

## The strategic-significance boundary

Not every category of facility deserves its own standing class. Carving one is a commitment — it says this category matters enough to warrant its own design basis, maintained over time. The boundary that justifies the carve is **strategic significance**: the category is elevated to a standing facility class when its failure would produce consequences that reach beyond the individual facility to national or systemic scale.

Strategic significance is itself a consequence judgment — the same consequence-first reasoning from Module 2, now applied one level up. You are not asking "could this plant have a bad day?" but "would the loss of facilities *of this kind* reach a consequence the nation cannot absorb?" A single fuel depot failing is a local emergency. The systematic loss of the category that moves a nation's fuel is a strategic one. The strategic-significance boundary is the line between those two, and where a category sits relative to it decides whether it earns a standing class.

Drawing that boundary is a real act of judgment, defensible and contestable — and as the next units show, exactly where the interesting disputes live.

## Why data centers are the newest class

The clearest recent illustration of a category *crossing* the strategic-significance boundary is the data center. For years, commercial cloud facilities were treated as ordinary commercial real estate — important, but not strategic infrastructure. That framing broke on **March 1, 2026**, when Iranian drones struck Amazon Web Services data centers in the United Arab Emirates and Bahrain — the first confirmed military attacks on hyperscale cloud facilities. Multiple availability zones went down at once, standard redundancy models failed, and the outages cascaded into banks, payment platforms, and other services across the region.

That event did to data centers what history had already done to power plants and fuel terminals: it demonstrated that the loss of the category produces consequences at strategic scale. In design-basis terms, data centers crossed the strategic-significance boundary in public, and the reasoning that carves them into a standing facility class is the same reasoning this module teaches — now applied to the newest member of the club. The examples in the next unit, including this one, are models for exactly the carve you may need to perform in your own sector.

## In your sector

Ask the class question of the sector you picked in Module 0. Is there a *category* of facility in it — not one plant, but a kind of plant — whose systematic loss would reach a national or systemic consequence? If yes, you are looking at a candidate facility class, and the strategic-significance boundary is the argument you would have to make to carve it. Hold that candidate; the map-your-sector unit at the end of this module is where you formalize it.
