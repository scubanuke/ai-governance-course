# Map Your Sector

> **Part of:** Bridge / Module 5 — the pivot point of the course

Everything so far has been preparation for this. You learned the method in the core and watched it carve real classes in the worked examples. Now you turn it on your own sector — the one you chose in Module 0 and have been answering *In Your Sector* prompts about ever since. When you finish this unit, you should know exactly which facility, or which facility class, you are taking into the capstone. This is where the course stops being something you read and becomes something you do.

## The mapping, step by step

Work these in order. Write your answers down — they are the raw material of your capstone, and later steps depend on earlier ones.

**1. Name the consequences that must not happen.** For your sector, state the specific, concrete outcomes that would be catastrophic — the consequence roots, in the language of Module 3. Be precise and be ruthless about the short list; a sector "protected" against twenty consequences is anchored on none.

**2. Locate the strategic-significance boundary.** Is there a *category* of facility in your sector — not one site, but a kind — whose systematic loss reaches a national or systemic consequence? Decide where the strategic-significance boundary falls for your sector, and which categories clear it. Recall from the worked examples that categories cross on different axes — a severe shared physical consequence, a strategic deliverability consequence, or a systemic dependency — so name *which axis* carries yours.

**3. Test applicability.** Run the applicability gate (the four-filter gate from the companion unit) against a candidate facility: does the class instrument actually apply here, or does this facility sit outside it? The gate is what keeps you honest about scope — it prevents both over-reach and false exclusion.

**4. Decide: inherit or derive.** Now the fork that defines your capstone. If a facility class already exists for your category, your capstone will *apply and tailor* that class's design basis to a specific facility. If no such class exists — which is the common and more valuable case — your capstone becomes **derive the facility class for a facility in my sector**, from the consequence root up. Either path is legitimate; the second is where the method earns its keep.

## What this pivot means for the rest of the course

The core insisted that you did not need a prebuilt facility class for your sector, and this is where that promise is honored. The energy carve-outs you just studied are models, not gates. If your sector is water, communications, transportation, healthcare, or finance and nothing has been carved for it yet, you are not blocked — you are exactly where the method is most useful, and your capstone is the carve itself.

Whichever fork you take, remember the core's central result: the facility where an AI operates has **two** design bases. Your capstone will require both — the facility-class design basis and the AI design basis, joined at the Bright Line. Do not leave this unit with only the facility half in mind.

## Hand-off to the branches

You now hold a concrete target: a named facility, or a facility class you will derive, in your own sector. That target is what you carry into the branch you are about to choose. The **OT track** will take it and have you *produce* a design basis and assessment. The **policy track** will take the same target and have you *produce* a consequence-and-governance analysis. Same facility, two lenses. Pick the branch that matches what you need to be able to produce — and bring your target with you.

## In your sector

Write the one-line version of your capstone now: *"I will take \_\_\_\_\_\_ [facility or facility class] in \_\_\_\_\_\_ [sector], and I will \_\_\_\_\_\_ [apply / derive] its design basis."* If you can complete that sentence, you are ready for the branch. If you cannot yet, the gap in the sentence tells you which of the four steps above still needs work.
