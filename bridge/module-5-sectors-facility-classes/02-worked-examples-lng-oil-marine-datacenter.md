# Worked Examples: LNG, Oil Marine Terminals, Data Centers

> **Part of:** Bridge / Module 5
> These are **worked examples** — models to imitate, not the only sectors the method serves. Each is drawn from the author's own carve-outs in the energy and infrastructure space. Read them for the *reasoning*, then run the same reasoning on your sector in the map-your-sector unit.

The last unit defined a facility class and the strategic-significance boundary that justifies carving one. This unit shows the carve actually being performed, three times, on three real categories. Watch the pattern more than the details: in each case the move is the same — establish the consequence profile, test it against the strategic-significance boundary, and, if it clears, elevate the category to a standing class with its own design basis.

## LNG terminals — a category promoted to a standing class

Liquefied-natural-gas import and export terminals were carved into a standing facility class because their consequence profile is both severe and shared across the category. The physical consequences are distinctive — large cryogenic inventories, the potential for vapor-cloud formation and dispersion, concentrated energy in a compact footprint — and they recur at every terminal of the type rather than being unique to one site. Above that sits the strategic layer: LNG terminals are chokepoints in national and international gas supply, so the systematic loss of the category reaches well beyond any single facility.

That combination — a severe, *shared* physical consequence plus strategic-scale supply consequence — is exactly what justifies promoting the category rather than treating each terminal one-off. The reasoning generalizes: when a category exhibits a common catastrophic consequence *and* strategic significance, it is a candidate for a standing class. (In the author's instrument set this is formalized as the LNG facility-class design basis; here it is the reasoning that matters.)

## Oil marine terminals — carved on strategic significance

Oil marine terminals make a subtler case, and they are the reason the next unit exists. Their site-level physical consequences — spill, fire, local environmental damage — are serious but, taken alone, might not clear the strategic-significance boundary; plenty of industrial facilities carry comparable local hazards without earning a standing class. What pushes oil marine terminals over the line is throughput concentration: a small number of terminals move a disproportionate share of a nation's oil, so their loss is a strategic supply consequence even when the site-level physical event is not uniquely catastrophic.

This is the important lesson in the example. The carve here rests primarily on the *strategic-significance* axis — the deliverability and supply consequence — rather than on a uniquely severe physical event. That is a legitimate basis for a class. It is also precisely where a genuine architectural tension appears: how to weigh strategic significance against deliverability-based consequence when they do not point the same way. The next unit takes that up as a live, unresolved question — this example is where you should first feel it.

## Data centers — the newest class, carved in public

Data centers are the freshest carve, and the one that happened in full view. For years they were treated as commercial real estate. Then, on **March 1, 2026**, Iranian drones struck AWS facilities in the UAE and Bahrain — the first confirmed military strikes on hyperscale cloud infrastructure. Multiple availability zones failed simultaneously, defeating the redundancy models the industry had trusted, and the outages cascaded into banks, payment systems, and platforms across the region.

Run the carve on that. The physical consequence is a facility loss; the strategic consequence is the simultaneous, cascading loss of the digital substrate that finance, logistics, and communications now run on — a systemic consequence that unmistakably clears the strategic-significance boundary. The 2026 strikes did not create the significance; they revealed it. Data centers are the newest standing facility class because a real event forced the consequence reasoning into the open, and the carve follows the same pattern as LNG and oil before it.

## Reading the pattern

Set the three side by side and the method is visible. LNG clears the boundary on *both* a severe shared physical consequence and strategic supply significance. Oil marine terminals clear it primarily on *strategic significance and deliverability*, with a more ordinary site-level hazard. Data centers clear it on *systemic, cascading dependency* revealed by attack. Three different routes across the same boundary — which is the point: the strategic-significance boundary is one line, but categories can reach it along different consequence axes. Recognizing which axis carries a given category is the skill you will need when you carve your own.

## In your sector

Take the candidate class you identified in the last unit. Which of these three does it most resemble — a severe shared physical consequence (like LNG), a strategic deliverability consequence with an ordinary site hazard (like oil marine), or a systemic dependency revealed by failure (like data centers)? Naming the axis your sector crosses on is the first real step of your own carve.
