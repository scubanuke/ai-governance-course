# The Founding Gap in Critical Infrastructure Protection

> **Part of:** Core / Module 0

This course begins with an absence. For all the plans, frameworks, and directives that govern how the United States protects its critical infrastructure, one discipline that makes safety engineering rigorous was never required of any sector but one. That missing requirement is the gap this whole course is built to close, and this unit names it precisely, because everything that follows is an answer to it.

## The discipline that only one sector was made to adopt

In nuclear power, before a facility is licensed, it must define what it is engineered to withstand. The regulator requires it to state, formally and in advance, the set of threats and conditions the plant's safety systems are designed against — a **design basis**, and for security specifically a **Design Basis Threat**. This is not a slogan. It is a regulated document with legal force: the operator must show its protective measures defeat a defined adversary, and the design basis is the yardstick every later safety and security argument is measured against. The discipline is old, demanding, and — crucially — mandatory.

No other critical-infrastructure sector carries this obligation. Not energy broadly, not water, not communications, not transportation, not financial services, not the data centers now underpinning everything else. They are protected, regulated, and in many cases well defended. But none of them was ever *required* to define a formal design basis in the nuclear sense — to state in advance the bounded set of conditions their critical functions are engineered to survive, and to be held to it.

## What the national framework asked for instead

This is not because no one thought about the problem. The United States built an elaborate architecture for critical-infrastructure protection: the National Infrastructure Protection Plan, the designation of sixteen critical-infrastructure sectors under Presidential Policy Directive 21, and most recently the 2024 National Security Memorandum on Critical Infrastructure Security and Resilience (NSM-22), which superseded PPD-21 and refreshed the framework for nation-state cyber threats, supply-chain risk, and more.

But look at what that architecture asks for. It is **risk-based** and **all-hazards**: assess your risks across the full range of hazards, prioritize by likelihood and impact, and manage them down. That is a genuinely useful posture, and it is not the same thing as a design basis. Risk management asks *what is likely and how bad, and where should I spend next?* A design basis asks a harder, more committing question: *what is the bounded set of conditions this system must be engineered to withstand, no matter the estimated likelihood, and can I demonstrate that it does?* One prioritizes across a spectrum of probabilities. The other draws a line and builds to it. The national framework institutionalized the first and left the second to nuclear alone.

## Why the gap is the whole point

The consequence of that choice is the founding gap. Most of critical-infrastructure protection has run for decades without the single discipline that makes safety engineering rigorous — the discipline of committing, in advance and in writing, to the envelope of conditions a critical function is designed to survive. Sectors defended themselves ably against the threats they could see, but without the design-basis backbone that forces the question of what *must not happen* and what the system is actually engineered to withstand.

That gap was tolerable, arguably, in a world of largely human-operated, physically bounded infrastructure. It becomes acute the moment autonomous and agentic AI is placed inside these systems — because now a facility can be driven toward its worst outcomes through a channel no risk register anticipated, and there is no design basis on file that says what the system was ever engineered to withstand. The absence that was quietly carried for years becomes the exposed nerve of the whole enterprise.

## What this course does about it

The course's claim is simple to state and demanding to execute: the design-basis discipline that nuclear was required to adopt should be extended — deliberately, as a method — to the governance of AI in every critical-infrastructure sector. Not because every sector should be regulated like nuclear, but because the *reasoning* nuclear was forced into is exactly the reasoning that makes AI in critical infrastructure governable at all. The rest of the core teaches that reasoning from the ground up. The branches and the capstone make you do it.

You will notice the nuclear and energy examples throughout. They are worked examples — the sectors where this discipline already lives or is being carved — not the boundary of where the method applies. The method is sector-agnostic. Which is why the very next thing this course asks you to do is choose the sector you will carry through all of it.

## In your sector

Before you pick, sit with the gap in your own terms. For the sector you have in mind, ask: *is there anywhere in it today where someone is required to state, in advance and in writing, the bounded set of conditions a critical function is engineered to withstand — and be held to it?* If the answer is yes, you have a rare head start. If the answer is no, you have just located, in your own sector, the exact gap this course exists to close.
