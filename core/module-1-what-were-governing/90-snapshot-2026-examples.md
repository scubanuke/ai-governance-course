# Snapshot: Generative, Agentic, and Autonomous AI in 2026

> **Part of:** Core / Module 1 — dated snapshot
> **Snapshot date:** 2026. This file is expected to age. It exists so the durable concept in `00-concept-autonomy-boundary.md` never has to carry dated examples. **Refresh only this file on each review cycle; leave the concept unit alone.**

The companion unit gave you the three categories and the boundary that matters. This one shows where real systems sit as of 2026, so the concept has something concrete to stand on. Read it as a photograph, not a definition — by the time you read it, the frontier will have moved, and that is by design.

## Where the three categories stand today

**Generative systems are everywhere and mostly advisory.** Large language and multimodal models drafting documents, summarizing reports, writing and reviewing code, and answering questions are, in 2026, ordinary infrastructure of knowledge work. In the terms of the concept unit they sit well back from the boundary: they produce outputs a human or a downstream system still has to act on. Their governance issues are real — accuracy, disclosure, data handling — but they are not, in themselves, the safety-critical crossing this course targets.

**Agentic systems crossed from novelty to operating model this year.** The notable shift in 2026 is that autonomous agents stopped being framed as experimental assistants and became a way of actually running work: systems that reason across data, call tools, and coordinate multi-step workflows with human oversight and authority. Enterprise agent platforms now let agents chain actions across communication tools, analytics systems, and document stores inside a single automated workflow; in security and DevOps, agents increasingly triage alerts and investigate issues that humans used to handle step by step. These systems are approaching the boundary — they *act* — and the live question is exactly the one the concept unit poses: what can those actions reach?

**Autonomous operation is arriving in high-consequence settings faster than governance for it.** The clearest signal came on **May 1, 2026**, when CISA, the NSA, and cybersecurity agencies from Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and the United Kingdom jointly published *Careful Adoption of Agentic AI Services* — the first coordinated multi-government security guidance aimed specifically at autonomous agent deployments. Its notable finding, for this course, is blunt: agentic systems are **already operating in critical infrastructure with insufficient governance.** The documented failure mode is precisely the far-side-of-the-boundary problem — an agent can misread a goal or generate a flawed plan and then execute it across many steps before a human notices.

## Why this snapshot matters to the boundary

Line these up against the concept unit and the pattern is clear. The capability that defines the boundary — self-directed action with reach into consequences that must not happen — is no longer hypothetical in 2026. It is being deployed, and by the admission of the governments that oversee critical infrastructure, it is being deployed ahead of the controls that should bound it. Regulation is moving in parallel: agentic systems placed in high-risk contexts such as critical infrastructure, healthcare, and financial services are being brought under mandatory risk-management, logging, and human-oversight obligations (the European Union's AI Act being the most developed example as of this snapshot). The picture is uneven across jurisdictions: the United States' 2023 executive order on AI, which had directed critical-infrastructure safety work, was rescinded in early 2025, leaving no equivalent federal directive — while some states moved the other way, California having enacted in 2024 a statutory mandate for recurring generative-AI risk analysis of its critical energy infrastructure. The rules are catching up unevenly; the deployments are not waiting.

That gap — autonomous reach arriving before the design basis that should bound it — is the 2026 face of the founding gap from Module 0. It is why this course is being written now rather than later.
