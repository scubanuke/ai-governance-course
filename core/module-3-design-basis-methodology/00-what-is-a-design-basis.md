# What a Design Basis Is

> **Part of:** Core / Module 3

This is the module the course is named for, and this unit defines its central object. You have the pieces already: Module 0 showed you the gap left by *not* having this discipline, and Module 2 taught you to start from consequence. A design basis is what those two ideas become when an engineering tradition makes them formal, mandatory, and testable. Here is what it is.

## The nuclear definition

In nuclear safety engineering, a **design basis** is the documented set of conditions and events a facility's safety systems are deliberately engineered to withstand — stated in advance, in writing, and treated as the reference against which the adequacy of the design is judged. It names the accidents, hazards, and threats the plant must handle, specifies what "handling" them means, and thereby defines the envelope of what the facility is built for. Everything inside that envelope, the design must defeat. The boundary of that envelope is drawn on purpose and defended as a judgment.

The power of the construct is what it does to an otherwise impossible problem. "Protect the plant from anything that could go wrong" is not an engineering specification; it is an anxiety. "Bring the plant to a safe, controlled state under this bounded, explicitly stated set of conditions" is an engineering specification — one you can design to, build to, test against, and audit. A design basis converts an unbounded fear into a bounded, demonstrable requirement. That conversion is the whole trick, and it is why nuclear safety is as rigorous as it is.

## The two moves every design basis makes

Strip a design basis to its mechanics and it always does two things, which are the next two units of this module.

First, it **anchors on consequence.** It begins from the outcomes that must not happen — the release, the meltdown, the loss of containment — and treats those as the fixed point everything else is derived from. The consequence is the root.

Second, it **bounds the adversary or the conditions.** It states, explicitly, the set of events and capabilities the design will defend against — and, just as explicitly, what falls outside that set. The bound is what makes the thing tractable.

Anchor on consequence; bound the threat. A design basis is those two disciplined moves, written down and committed to. Units 01 and 02 take them one at a time.

## Why it ports to other sectors — and to AI

Nothing in that definition is specifically nuclear. "Nuclear" supplies the worked example — the releases and reactor accidents that gave the discipline its first home — but the *method* is domain-general. Any system with an outcome that must not happen, and a bounded set of conditions that could cause it, can be given a design basis. That is the wager of this whole course: the discipline nuclear was required to adopt (recall the founding gap from Module 0, where nuclear was the only sector made to do this) is exactly the discipline every critical-infrastructure sector needs as AI enters its operations.

When the object of the design basis becomes an AI system rather than a reactor, the two moves do not change. You still anchor on the consequences that must not happen, and you still bound what you are defending against. What changes is only the content of the second move — the adversary envelope for a model is not the adversary envelope for a pump — and that single divergence is the seam this module builds toward in its keystone unit. For now, hold the definition: a design basis is a written, bounded, consequence-anchored commitment to what a system is engineered to withstand. Learn to derive one, and you can derive it for anything, including the AI.

## In your sector

Take the must-not-happen outcome you have been carrying since Module 2. Ask the design-basis question of it directly: *if I had to write down, in advance, the bounded set of conditions a facility in my sector is engineered to withstand so that this outcome never occurs — could I? Does such a document exist?* Whether the answer is "yes, and here it is" or "no, nothing like that exists," you have just located where the design-basis discipline stands in your sector today.
