# From DB to DBA to DBA-MA

> **Part of:** Core / Module 3

You now have the two moves — anchor on consequence, bound the adversary. This unit shows the method in motion across widening classes of threat, and it introduces the vocabulary the branches and the capstone use. The progression runs **DB → DBA → DBA-MA**: the general discipline, its classic instantiation, and its extension into deliberate adversarial and military action. The method never changes across these steps. Only the class of initiating event it is pointed at gets wider.

## DB — the discipline

The **design basis (DB)** is the general construct from unit 00: a written, bounded, consequence-anchored commitment to what a system is engineered to withstand. It is the parent idea. Everything below is the same idea aimed at a specific family of things that could go wrong. Keep the DB in view as the method; the next two terms are that method instantiated.

## DBA — the classic instantiation

The **design basis accident (DBA)** is where nuclear engineering first made the discipline concrete, and it is the worked example that gives the whole method its credibility. A DBA is a postulated accident — an equipment failure, a pipe break, a loss of coolant, a natural hazard — that the plant's safety systems are specifically designed to bring to a safe, controlled state. The facility is engineered so that even when the postulated accident occurs, the consequence root — the release, the meltdown — does not. The DBA is the design basis applied to *accidents*: things that fail, break, or occur without anyone intending them.

Notice what the DBA assumes about intent: essentially nothing. Its initiating events are accidental and natural. That was the right scope for the era it was built in, and it is also its limit — an accident-shaped design basis does not, on its own, account for an adversary who is *trying* to reach the consequence root.

## DBA-MA — the extension to deliberate action

The **design basis accident — military action (DBA-MA)** is this course's extension of the tradition into the adversarial domain: the same method, the same two moves, now pointed at deliberate action — cyber attack, sabotage, and up to military or kinetic assault — rather than accident. The consequence root is unchanged; a facility's must-not-happen outcomes are the same whether reached by a broken pipe or a determined adversary. What changes is the envelope: the DBA-MA bounds an *intentional* adversary, with capability, intent, and persistence, where the classic DBA bounded blind accidents.

This is the step that carries the design-basis discipline from safety into security, and from the nuclear plant out toward the rest of critical infrastructure. In the energy sector, this course's worked examples build out a family of these instruments — facility-class design bases and their adversary-and-military-action extensions — as concrete demonstrations of the method. Treat those as illustrations, not as the boundary of where the method applies: the DB→DBA→DBA-MA progression is sector-agnostic, and your own sector's version is exactly what the bridge module and the capstone will have you derive.

## Why the progression matters for AI

Follow the widening: from accidents (DBA), to deliberate human adversaries and military action (DBA-MA). Each step keeps the consequence root and widens the adversary envelope to a new class of initiating cause. An autonomous AI operating inside a facility is the next thing that has to be brought into this picture — and it does not fit cleanly as just another entry in the facility's envelope, because the way an adversary reaches the consequence *through the AI* is unlike any physical or military path the facility's design basis was drawn against.

That is the hand-off. Once you can derive a DBA-MA for a facility, you have the facility's design basis. The facility where an AI operates needs a **second** one — for the AI itself — built by this same method but with its own, very different envelope. The next unit, the keystone of this module, makes that argument in full: two design bases, one shared consequence root, two divergent envelopes, and the Bright Line seam between them.

## In your sector

Take the root and first envelope you sketched in the last two units and place them on the progression. Is the envelope you drew an *accident* envelope (DBA-shaped) or an *adversary* envelope (DBA-MA-shaped)? Most people instinctively draw one and forget the other. Note which you defaulted to — and what a facility in your sector would need to add to cover the class you left out. Carry that into the keystone.
