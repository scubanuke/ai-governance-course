# Mapping to ISA/IEC 62443

> **Part of:** Branch / OT Track

An OT practitioner does not get to invent a governance world from scratch. There is already a dominant standard for securing industrial control systems — ISA/IEC 62443 — and any credible design-basis work has to say where it aligns with that standard and where it leaves a gap. This unit follows a systematic gap analysis of 62443 against AI-specific risk, so you can position your design basis as a complement to the standard rather than a competitor to it.

## What 62443 is

ISA/IEC 62443 is a four-part series for industrial-automation-and-control-system security. The 1-x parts set concepts and terminology; the 2-x parts govern asset-owner and service-provider policies (including supplier security requirements); the 3-x parts work at the system level (a risk-assessment methodology and the system security requirements); and the 4-x parts work at the component level (a secure-development lifecycle and technical component requirements). Two constructs tie it together: the **zone-and-conduit model** — assets grouped into zones sharing security requirements, connected by defined conduits — and **Security Levels SL 1 through SL 4**, scaling from protection against casual violation up to protection against sophisticated state-sponsored attackers. Its requirements organize under seven **Foundational Requirements**, from access control through system integrity to resource availability.

Know this structure cold, because the gap analysis is conducted requirement by requirement against it.

## Where the design-basis approach aligns

Start with the alignment, because it is real and it matters for tone: 62443 is not wrong, and your work sits *on top of* it. Its defense-in-depth and security-level machinery protect against external threats to both the deterministic and the AI layers of a system. Its use-control requirement gives a conceptual home for restricting autonomous operation. Its resource-availability requirement transfers to AI with only modest extension — the single "minor" gap in the whole analysis. And the bright-line philosophy — scale protection to consequence — is exactly 62443's own risk-based instinct. The design basis extends 62443's spirit into a domain it predates.

## Where the gap is

The gap has a single root, and everything else follows from it: 62443's entire technical architecture assumes **deterministic behavior.** Component requirements, security-level assignment, integrity verification — all presume that a component does the same thing given the same conditions. Cognitive AI does not, and so the assumption fails in specific, nameable places. Of twelve 62443 elements assessed, the analysis found **nine significant gaps, two moderate, one minor, and none fully addressed** — a result worth stating plainly to anyone who assumes 62443 already covers AI.

The significant gaps cluster in three areas. First, **identity and integrity**: an AI component has no stable auditable identity — a retrained or fine-tuned model "looks the same but behaves differently" — and hash verification is defeated by the fact that "a correctly hashed model can still be a corrupted model if the training data was poisoned." Integrity of the artifact is not integrity of the behavior. Second, the **zone-and-conduit model misses AI inference**: a model that takes input from one zone and affects another creates an implicit data flow the conduit model never captured — "any AI system that receives inputs from one security zone and produces outputs that affect another zone is effectively a conduit and should be governed as such." Third, the **supply chain and development lifecycle** are silent on AI: no foundation-model provenance, no fine-tuning lineage, no ML-development-lifecycle security, and the component requirements offer no method to assign a security level to a system that is "99.9% correct and 0.1% adversarially exploitable." Use control, likewise, has no analog for the bright line or the command broker — the enumerable action space it assumes does not exist for a generative system.

## The framing that keeps it constructive

Two disciplines make this a contribution rather than an attack. First, the recommended actions sort into three kinds — **extend** an existing requirement, **interpret** how a current requirement applies, or write a **new instrument** where nothing fits — so each gap comes with a proportionate remedy rather than a demand to rebuild the standard. Second, the framing is explicit and generous: *"this is not a deficiency in 62443; it is a consequence of the standard being developed before generative AI became a control-system concern."* That is the posture to adopt in your own deliverable. You are not overturning the standard OT lives by; you are naming precisely where the determinism assumption stops holding and proposing the smallest change that closes each gap — for instance, mandating runtime behavioral monitoring for AI components at the higher security levels, or treating cross-zone inference as a governed conduit.

## For your capstone facility

Take your facility's AI and walk it against the seven foundational requirements. Find one requirement it cannot satisfy because of non-determinism — identity, integrity, restricted data flow are the usual first hits — and write the gap the way the analysis does: name the requirement, state why the determinism assumption fails there, and specify whether you would extend, interpret, or add an instrument. That single well-formed gap statement is how your design basis earns its place alongside 62443.
