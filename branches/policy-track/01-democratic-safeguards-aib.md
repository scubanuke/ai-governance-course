# The Autonomous Influence Broker

> **Part of:** Branch / Policy Track

The OT branch had a canonical machine — the Aurora Disruptor. The policy branch has a canonical *threat*: the Autonomous Influence Broker. It is the clearest case of an AI system crossing the boundary from Module 1 into a safety-critical function, except that here the "facility" is the democratic process and the must-not-happen consequence is a manipulated electorate. This unit defines it, shows how it works, and names the governance primitive that answers it.

## What an Autonomous Influence Broker is

An **Autonomous Influence Broker (AIB)** is an AI system — or a coordinated network of them — that detects, amplifies, and directs influence at scale "with sufficient autonomy that no human principal individually authorizes the system's specific outputs." The word *broker* is exact: it does not merely generate content, it brokers influence, matching persuasive pressure to targets across a population. Its defining trait is autonomy of the individual act — a human may switch the system on and set its goal, but no human approves the specific message sent to the specific citizen.

Its operation is a four-stage pipeline run as a closed loop: **aggregate → profile → generate → deliver**, with delivery feedback flowing back into profiling so the system learns. This is what the source calls an Autonomous Influence Operation, and it is what dissolves the old tradeoff between precision and scale. Broadcast propaganda had scale without precision; classic intelligence work had precision without scale. The AIB delivers "millions of individually adapted variations" at once — "engineering a unique persuasion strategy for each person."

## Why it is a governance problem, not a marketing one

Three things separate an AIB from ordinary micro-targeted advertising, and together they make it a Bright Line problem. The **profiling** is individual and behavioral — a model of one person's traits, trust networks, and predicted responses, not a demographic segment. The **content** is uniquely generated per target and adapts on engagement feedback, and its synthetic origin is effectively undetectable to the recipient. And the **autonomy** means the optimizer "will find and exploit persuasion strategies that a human operator would recognize as manipulative," at a scale that makes after-the-fact correction impossible.

The consequence is concrete. In a district decided by a few thousand votes, profiling the persuadable slice and shifting a fraction of them can flip the outcome — and the reported persuasion effect sizes sit within that range. The person on the receiving end "does not experience this as propaganda; the individual data points feel organic, the pattern is engineered." And because no human authorized any specific act against any specific citizen, accountability simply vanishes — the harm is real and attributable to no one. That combination — irreversible democratic consequence plus non-verifiable autonomous reach — is exactly the far side of the boundary the whole course targets.

## The Aggregation Threshold: where the line is crossed

The policy analysis pins the crossing to a nameable point: the **Aggregation Threshold**, "the point at which an AI system's cognitive synthesis of legally obtained data crosses into functional mass surveillance." The insight is that each data point is individually innocuous and legally obtained; it is the *synthesis* that becomes surveillance. The threshold is grounded in re-identification science, the Carpenter "mosaic" doctrine, and privacy-framework practice, and it is calibrated to behavioral *prediction* — a stronger condition than mere re-identification. It is defined so it can be audited at three layers: the deterministic inputs (verifiable), the cognitive outputs (red-teamed), and the operational configuration (to defeat evasion). Treat the specific figures — population triggers, category counts — as provisional proposals pending standards rulemaking, not settled law; the durable idea is that there is a definable line where aggregation becomes surveillance, and it can be made auditable.

## The Influence Broker: the same primitive, new domain

The governance answer is the Module 4 primitive in a new setting. Just as the Command Broker interposes a human between an AI recommendation and a physical action, the **Influence Broker** interposes a mandatory human gate between the pipeline's **generation** and **delivery** stages. The AI may aggregate, profile, and generate autonomously — but "the delivery pipeline requires human authentication to execute, and this authentication cannot be bypassed through software configuration, policy override, or administrative action." It is the same design principle as the kill-chain broker: "the firing circuit passes through a switch that requires a human hand." Its second job is to restore accountability — so that when someone asks who authorized an influence operation, "the answer must be a person, not an algorithm."

A note on names: the *broker* the AIB performs is the threat (brokering influence); the *Influence Broker* is the human safeguard that breaks it. Same word, opposite roles — keep them straight and the framework is clear.

## For your capstone analysis

If your sector touches public opinion, markets, or any decision made at population scale, ask the AIB question of it: could an autonomous system aggregate, profile, generate, and deliver influence toward your sector's must-not-happen consequence with no human authorizing the individual acts? If so, locate the equivalent of the Aggregation Threshold (where does synthesis become surveillance in your domain?) and specify the Influence Broker gate that would restore a human — and accountability — to the loop. That pairing is the core of an influence-side governance analysis.
