# Three Forms of Deterrence

> **Part of:** Branch / Policy Track

A governance analysis eventually has to answer a strategic question: what would actually stop an adversary? "Deterrence" is the usual word, but it is often used loosely. This unit gives it structure — the three forms of deterrence, drawn from the sector deterrence policy work — and shows how a design-basis architecture produces each. It also sets up the standards-gap argument that closes the branch, because the punchline is that deterrence without standards is largely unattainable.

## Deterrence, defined for infrastructure

Deterrence is "the condition in which a potential adversary concludes that the costs and risks of an attack outweigh its anticipated benefits." For critical infrastructure the mechanism is not nuclear-style retaliation; it is getting the adversary to believe an attack "will not produce the strategic outcome it seeks." There are three distinct ways to produce that belief, and a serious analysis keeps them separate.

## The three forms

**Deterrence by punishment.** The adversary refrains because an attack "will be attributed and lead to unacceptable consequences — military, economic, or diplomatic." It requires attribution capability, response authority, and credibility. It operates at the state level and is "the form least directly influenced by the physical security posture of individual facilities." A nation may have abundant punishment capacity and still be attacked, because punishment addresses the aftermath, not the attempt.

**Deterrence by denial.** The adversary "concludes the attack will not succeed" because the target is hardened, defended, or redundant. This is the form "directly produced by physical security investment," and it is the one that matters most to the standards argument. Denial does not depend on catching or punishing anyone; it depends on making success unlikely enough that the attempt is not worth mounting.

**Deterrence by resilience.** The adversary attacks and "fails to achieve strategic effect" because "the target system recovers faster than the adversary's campaign can sustain." It requires redundancy, recovery architecture, and mutual assistance. Resilience and denial are complementary: hardening lowers the severity of the initiating event that resilience must then absorb. These are layers, not a ranking — a strong posture uses all three.

## How a design basis produces each

The design-basis method feeds all three forms, but most directly denial. A design basis specifies what a facility must withstand; building to it is precisely what makes an attack unlikely to succeed. Mandatory design-basis standards do something subtler and more powerful, though: they "establish a floor" across a whole set of facilities. The key mechanism is a belief the adversary can no longer form — "the adversary cannot reliably identify the least-hardened facility in the covered set because the framework prevents the most extreme implementation gaps from existing." Denial, in other words, is a property of the *set*, not of any one well-run site.

For resilience, design-basis hardening reduces the size of the shock the recovery architecture has to absorb. For punishment, the design basis contributes less directly — attribution and response are national capabilities — but a documented, bounded threat envelope strengthens the case that a given attack was a deliberate act against a known standard.

## Why deterrence without standards fails

Here is the argument the whole branch has been building toward, and it is sharper than it first appears. Denial requires the adversary to *believe* the target is hardened — and belief needs "observable, consistent, verifiable evidence." Voluntary guidelines produce none of that: implementation is inconsistent, unenforced, and invisible to the government and allies who would need to see it. So a sector defended only by voluntary measures "presents an inconsistent set of targets, allowing the adversary to select the least-hardened facilities." The hardening might even be real at many sites, but without a mandatory floor the adversary can always hunt for the gap.

The analysis calls this being "deterrence-incoherent across sectors," and the electricity–gas seam is the vivid example: electricity operates under mandatory physical-security standards while the gas that fuels a large share of electricity generation runs on voluntary guidelines — even though "the adversary does not organize its targeting by regulatory jurisdiction; the regulatory framework does." The evidentiary record it draws on — a major pipeline ransomware shutdown, the Nord Stream attacks, the systematic targeting of Ukraine's energy system — is offered as what the absence of denial looks like in practice. The conclusion is structural: without a mandatory standards regime, deterrence "is structurally impossible to achieve," because the belief that produces denial cannot be formed from evidence that does not exist.

## For your capstone analysis

For your capstone sector, take each form in turn. Who holds the punishment capacity, and does it reach your adversary? What would denial require, and is there a mandatory floor — or could an adversary shop for your least-hardened facility? What resilience already exists, and how much shock would it have to absorb if denial failed? Name which form your sector actually relies on today, and whether that reliance survives the "least-hardened facility" test. That assessment is the strategic core of your governance analysis, and it hands directly to the standards-gap argument next.
